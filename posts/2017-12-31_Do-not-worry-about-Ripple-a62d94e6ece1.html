Do not worry about Ripple

Run by banks?

Ripple is not necessarily run by banks. It just turned out that the concept of assets in Ripple (IOUs for various fiat currencies) very accurately represents what bank money really is. Originally (Ripple was around before Bitcoin) the idea was that anyone would be able to run a Ripple gateway and issue IOUs (by accepting people’s deposits and processing withdrawals in exchange for those IOUs). But due to problems with reversed fiat transactions and various regulations, it turned out that the existing banks are actually the perfect gateways.

What is the function of XRP?

My understanding is that XRP (native currency of Ripple) has 2 functions in such system:

  1. A small amount of it is required to create IOUs (to prevent spamming)
  2. It can play a role of intermediate asset if there is no direct market between IOUs. For example, if there is Citi USD IOUs and Chase USD IOUs, and there is no direct swap, you might be able to swap one for XRP and then XRP for another

Consensus mechanism centralised?

People often criticise Ripple’s consensus mechanism. When you set up a node (I tried to do that back in 2014, but gave up after 2 weeks of trying, though I got Bitcoin and Ethereum POC going in no time), you specify the list of nodes that you “trust” (or just take the default which contains bunch of sX.ripple.com servers), and then you definition of finality of certain transaction depends on your list of “trusted” servers. If your list is too non-standard, you might see different picture of finality from others. Therefore, most people simply use the default, because it is a “Shelling point”

Is it good or bad?

I think adoption of Ripple allows banks (and their customers) to better deal with bank money (IOUs) especially in an event of a credit crisis, where value of various IOUs diverge between each other and from the high-powered money (cash and central bank money). Traditionally, accounting systems in banks (and other enterprises) simply assume that “money is money”, leaving out the nuances that most money is actually someone’s liability. If you start using Ripple to improve your accounting, it is less likely you will be sending money into a “black hole” (bankrupted counter-party), as it happened to many in the first days after Lehman filed for bankruptcy.

Competition?

And I also think that Ripple is not a competition for Bitcoin or Ethereum, or any other public cryptocurrencies. Ripple is a platform for IOU management. It can also have IOUs for Ethereum and Bitcoin, if you will. But Bitcoin and Ethereum, if they stay de-centralised, will still be like high-powered money in relation to those IOUs. The synergy is possible.
